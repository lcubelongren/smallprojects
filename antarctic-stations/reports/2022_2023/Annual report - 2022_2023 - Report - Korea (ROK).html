      Disciplines
      A study on the establishment of multiple application of extant Foraminifera for monitoring of Antarctic marine environment and climate change
      Climate studies
      Main activity / remark
      In order to study the diversity and distribution of modern foraminifera from King George Island in Antarctica, a field survey was conducted in 2022/2023 austral summer, from the waters near the King Sejong Station, King George Island, Antarctica. Sediment and plankton samples were obtained from Marian Cove, Potter Cove, Maxwell Bay, and the coast of Barton and Weaver Peninsula. By examining 125 μm sieved samples under a stereomicroscope in the King Sejong Station, we could recognized diverse invertebrates including copepods, amphipods, isopods, nematods, ostracods, polychaetes, gastropods, tunicates, hydromedusae. Foraminifera were particularly abundant in sediment samples, 20 genera were identified (Nodulina, Cornuspira, Rosalina, Pyrgo, Psammosphaera, Astrammina, Hippocrepinella Lagenammina, Cassidulinoides, Globocassidulina, Spiroplectammina, Cibicides, Miliammina, Psammophaga, Lagenammnia, Gordiospira, Fissurina, Portatrochammina, Tholosina, Adercotryma). Additionally, by analyzing all samples obtained through this survey, we intend to secure data on the species composition, distribution, DNA barcode sequence, and protein mass spectra of foraminifera inhabiting the waters near the King Sejong Station. The data is expected to be used as a basis for Antarctic marine environmental monitoring and climate change studies using foraminifera in the future.

      Ecology
      Main activity / remark
      our research group studied the ecological response to global warming with the model plant species named Deschampsis antarctica. In order to observe the annual growth of D. antarctica, we selected ~50 individuals for a long-term observations at Potter Cove, near the Marian Cove, and near the seals’ village shelter. To measure the weight of D. antarctica in non-destructive ways, we developed a software called GreenScale. This program estimates the weight of D. antarctica by taking a picture of the species and calculating the number of pixels that make up the individual from the image. By repeating this task every year, we hope to predict the growth patterns of D. antarctica that would result from the influence of global warming.

      Project Name/Number
      Disciplines
      Field survey of interaction of Antarctic vegetation components under the climate change
      Climate studies
      Main activity / remark
      The aim of this field research, conducted from January 13 to February 5, 2023, in the vicinity of the Antarctic King Sejong Station, was to collect basic biological response data for modeling the response of major terrestrial organisms to environmental changes. The research team investigated plant-microbe interactions of Antarctic flora, sampled vegetation on the Barton Peninsula and Weaver Peninsula, and monitored the growth and physiological responses of Sejong Station's surrounding flora to changing environmental conditions. To achieve these goals, the team collected a variety of biocrust samples and pathogen-infected plants, isolated and cultured fungi and bacteria, and installed open-top chambers on Weaver Peninsula to simulate the effects of climate change on moss growth. Additionally, the team prepared samples for cell protective metabolite analysis, based on the seasonal gene regulation mechanism of the Antarctic moss. This field research is expected to contribute to a better understanding of the diversity and ecosystem changes of the Antarctic region, as well as provide valuable data for modeling the response of terrestrial organisms to environmental changes.

      Disciplines
      First whole genome sequencing and evolutionary adaptations in Antarctic Collembola (Cryptopygus antarctic)
      Biological sciences - other
      Main activity / remark
      Collembola are distributed in various environments globally, occupying a critical position in understanding the adaptive mechanisms of invertebrates. Specifically, Antarctic Collembola, which reside in the extremely cold and arid conditions, have not yet had their transcriptomeic and genomic information elucidated, despite their considerable importance. In this study, Antarctic Collembola (Cryptopygus antarcticus) were observed and collected from diverse habitats, such as lakes, mosses and glaciers, in the vicinity of King Sejong Station during the 2022/23 austral summer season. Samples were obtained for the purpose of isolating genomic and transcriptomic data. The findings of this research are anticipated to provide valuable insights into the genetic evolution and adaptation of Antarctic Collembola to polar environments.
